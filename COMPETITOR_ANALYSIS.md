🧠 ***Apps/Websites That Use Compatibility Questionnaires***

These platforms use questionnaires (either required or optional) to power algorithmic matching based on personality, values, preferences, lifestyle, and psychological characteristics.

1) eHarmony

Approach: Extensive compatibility questionnaire + algorithmic matching.

Questionnaire: Users complete a Compatibility Quiz (80+ questions) at signup that dives into personality traits, values, emotional needs, communication style, relationship goals, and life priorities; this forms the basis of its unique personality profile.

How it's used: Matches are algorithmically generated — you don’t browse swipes, you receive curated suggestions from the system once the quiz is complete.

Examples (themes):

Attitudes toward relationships and why you think you’re single.

Communication style indicators.

Personal preferences and life priorities (likes/dislikes, temperament, etc.).

Some screens even include visual preference choices (pictures you select between) which help model personality traits.

Assessment: One of the most comprehensive compatibility systems in the mainstream dating world, often requiring ~20+ minutes of responses before matching begins.

2) OkCupid

Approach: Uses multiple‑choice “Match Questions” to generate a Match Percentage score between users based on shared answers.

Questionnaire Size: OkCupid’s pool contains thousands of questions, and the app encourages answering between ~50–100 to optimize match results.

Core Mechanics:

For each question you answer, you set:

your answer,

acceptable answers from a partner,

and how important the question is to you.

Matches are scored based on how aligned these answers are.

Examples (representative topics):

Views on religion and beliefs (e.g., role of God in life).

Relationship parameters (open relationship debate).

Lifestyle preferences (e.g., pets, smoking).

Communication and affection styles.

Social and political views.

Assessment: OkCupid arguably has the widest and most customizable questionnaire system available — though in practice users often answer far fewer than the total available pool.

3) Match.com

Approach: Offers “Match Questions” and profile prompts that assess values, lifestyle, and personal preferences.

Questionnaire Use: These are used to influence the algorithm’s matching logic and refine recommendations but are not generally mandatory.

Examples: Questions often ask about:

Relationship goals,

Lifestyle habits,

Personality attributes and priorities.

Assessment: More extensive than swipe‑only apps but less rigorous than eHarmony or OkCupid.

4) EliteSingles

Approach: Targets professionals and long‑term relationships with a personality test built on Big Five traits (openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, neuroticism).

Questionnaire: Users complete a structured assessment incorporated into the onboarding process; this feeds into match recommendations.

Example Themes:

Personality trait indicators,

Long‑term goals,

Lifestyle habits.

Assessment: More systematic than consumer apps, tailor‑made for serious daters and long‑term compatibility.

5) Plenty of Fish

Approach: Offers a “Chemistry Test” that analyzes personality traits and relationship needs.

Questionnaire Size: Typically shorter than eHarmony or OkCupid but still offerings that feed into match triggers.

Assessment: Medium tier — not as deep as OkCupid’s huge pool, but still more structured than swipe‑only apps.

6) Tribal (newer app highlighted in news)

Approach: Uses a scientific compatibility‑based questionnaire with 44 items for dating and 33 for friendship, tied to functional values and communication styles.

Unique Feature: Visual “compatibility map” and blurred photo period to emphasize values before visuals.

Assessment: Appears to be one of the most questionnaire‑centric modern dating apps, but still emerging.

📱 Popular Apps That Do Not Use Extensive Questionnaires

These platforms either use minimal preference fields or rely on behavioral signals, swiping, and prompt responses rather than full‑length questionnaires.

7) Hinge

Approach: Focuses on profile prompts, photos, and machine learning behavior tracking.

Questionnaire: There is no traditional compatibility quiz; instead, Hinge uses user data (prompts, likes, and interactions) to support its “Most Compatible” algorithm.

Examples: Prompts like:

“Two truths and a lie…”

“A non‑negotiable…”
These serve more as personality displays than formal matching questions.

Assessment: Hinge prioritizes expressive profile content and AI behavior data over structured questionnaires.

8) Tinder

Approach: Pure swipe platform with optional profile info and short preferences.

Questionnaire: None — there’s no compatibility quiz at signup; matching is largely based on proximity, swipes, and, in some regions, basic preferences (age, gender).

Assessment: The most minimal in terms of algorithmic compatibility, focusing on ease of use and quick matching.

9) Bumble

Approach: Swipe‑based; no formal long compatibility questionnaire.

Questionnaire: Bumble includes some preferences (like relationship type, intentions), but no systematic compatibility survey.

Assessment: Similar to Tinder; minimal formal compatibility assessment.

10) Feeld

Approach: Niche focus (ethical non‑monogamy, alternative relationship models).

Questionnaire: Users select from sexuality, gender, and desire options, which influences what matches they see — but there is no deep personality compatibility quiz.

Assessment: Compatibility here is more about preference filtering than psychological matching.

📝 Takeaways

📌 Algorithmic compatibility systems — like eHarmony and OkCupid — rely heavily on questionnaires to estimate relational compatibility and are best for long‑term or values‑based matching.

📌 Swipe‑based platforms — like Tinder and Bumble — minimize entry barriers with no deep questionnaires, prioritizing fast matchmaking and usability.

📌 Hybrid approaches — platforms like Hinge and Tribal mix behavioral data or tailored questionnaires with profile content to strike a balance.

📌 Many compatibility systems let users influence how matches are calculated (e.g., OkCupid’s importance rating on answers), not just what answers are given.
